A series of 12 'Settings' screens - includes overrides and variants. Inspired by Samsung's One UI design guidelines

Design System

What is it?

A design system is a systematic standard or approach to product design/development

There are different definitions and interpretations of what exactly a design system is. Since they come in various styles, contexts and depths I'm going to use my own definition. A design system is a systematic standard or approach to product design/development.

Design systems could be used to expedite tasks, effectively communicate between teams, and track a product's growth. Having a design system on hand isn't just a guideline or a rigid tool, rather it's a collection of components with a large cope of possibility and efficiency.

Organizations like Adobe, Google and Atlassian all have design systems

Creation

I decided to create a design system because:

  • • I wanted to practice using a more systematic approach toward designing components
  • • I wanted to have ready-to-use components on hand for future projects
  • • I wanted to delve into a project that I could iterate and improve upon

Method

Research

In order to increase my base knowledge on what a design system is, I decided to look through various design systems and frameworks available for viewing on the web. Included were Google's Material Design, NASA Graphics Standards Manual, and the Atomic Design methodlogy.

Material Design

Google's Material Design system is extensive and would be quite overwhelming for me to emulate due to the sheer amount of elements and scope of the system. That being said, there is still a lot I can take from this system such as the simplicity of one font (Roboto) and the availability for icons (Figma plugin 'Material Design Icons' by smrkv)

NASA Graphics Standards Manual

While there wasn't a whole lot I took from NASA's design system, in regard to my personal design system, it served as a good reminder that NASA, despite being a government administration, is also an organization that embraces design.

Atomic Design

Atomic Design isn't so much a design 'system' as much as it's a framework for organizing the many elements one may create when developing a complex design system. I feel it's a resource for planning the long-term development of the design system and thinking about intelligent naming conventions for the components

Atomic design overview from Roberto Moreno Celta's article

Scaling down

Due to my familiarity with Galaxy phones and the layout of the system, I decided to begin my design system based off of Samsung's One UI Design Guidelines. Given the constraints of the project (1 contributor, time, etc.) I decided to begin the system with the components that make up the 'Settings' screens.

Aside from the high-level challenges such as identifying the grid system, screen dimensions, font-sizes, etc., It was important that I reference guidelines and take inventory of the components involved in Settings.

Components

Settings components

Upon viewing the ONE UI Design Guidelines I identified three classes or types of components for Settings. The classes I identified were App (Settings), Home (phone's home screen - will house the "Settings" app icon), and System (or Operating System)

App (Settings)

App components exist within the settings application. This class of components is made up of app bar, list items, lists, icons, images, etc.

Home

Home components exist on the phone's homescreen. This class of components includes labeled app icons, dialogue menus, etc.

System

System components are elements that make up the phone's operating system. This class of components includes system navigation bars, bottom bars, system icons, etc.

Components I used to design the screens I've created so far. Most of these components contain smaller components within themselves (think Atomic Design) and are created with overrides and variants in mind.
